Samsung's semiconductor engineers pasted source code, meeting transcripts, and chip yield data into ChatGPT inside a single month. Only 37 percent of organizations have a governance policy worth the name, while somewhere between 78 and 86 percent of employees are already using AI tools their employer never approved. Gartner found that 69 percent of organizations either suspect or have confirmed prohibited gen AI use happening inside their walls.

This is not a risk scenario for some future board deck; it is happening right now, in your organization, probably this week.

## The instinct that makes it worse

Most leaders react the same way: lock it down, ban ChatGPT, block the URLs, and send a stern email from Legal hoping the problem goes back underground.

I once watched a technician bypass a connector inspection because the approved tool was back-ordered for six weeks. He used a flashlight and his fingernail, the connector passed, and three months later that aircraft had an in-flight electrical fault traced back to exactly that connector. The military calls this kind of workaround field expedience; in the enterprise, we call it shadow AI, and the lesson is identical. Prohibition without enablement produces risk, not compliance, and it moves that risk somewhere you can no longer see it.

## Why this is governance, not IT

Think about what actually happens when an employee pastes a customer record into an unapproved tool. You may have just breached Loi 25 in Quebec or PIPEDA federally, created a decision trail nobody can audit, and lost any answer to the question of where that data now lives. Whatever compliance posture your team spent years building has quietly been gutted in the time it took to hit Ctrl+V.

No firewall rule fixes that, no memo from the CISO fixes that, and structure is the only thing that does.

The data on structure is striking. Organizations that offer approved AI tools with clear usage guidelines see unauthorized use collapse by 89 percent, because the vast majority of shadow AI is not malicious but pragmatic. People want to do better work faster, and when you give them a sanctioned path, they take it.

## What an AI Management System actually buys you

ISO 42001 is not paperwork for its own sake; it is the operating system that makes AI adoption safe enough to scale, which is a different problem than making it fast. An AI Management System gives you back three things shadow AI silently took: visibility into which tools are in use and how, accountability for decisions assisted or made by AI, and a documented trail that holds up under a regulator's or an auditor's questions.

Starting is less daunting than most organizations assume. Sort your AI tools into tiers based on what data they can touch, map the roles and workflows that sit against each tier, and teach people the boundaries in language they actually understand rather than in legalese. Then run the loop: monitor, adjust, repeat. The first version will be imperfect, and that is fine, because an imperfect governance system beats an invisible one every day of the week.

## Who wins the adoption race

The organizations that will win the AI adoption race are not the ones with the most aggressive deployment targets; they are the ones that build the governance scaffolding first, so that when people inevitably reach for AI tools, a framework is already there to catch them. Not to catch them doing something wrong, but to catch them the way a good system catches people, with support, structure, and a clear path forward.

The alternative is what we already have: a majority of your workforce using tools you cannot see, processing data you cannot track, and making decisions you cannot audit. That is not innovation; it is flying blind with the weather radar turned off.

If you lead an organization in Quebec or anywhere in Canada, the federal regulatory vacuum means you cannot wait for Ottawa. ISO 42001 exists today, the frameworks are ready today, and the only real question is whether you put them in place before or after the incident that turns governance from a strategic choice into an emergency.

The question worth sitting with is whether the next eighteen months give your organization enough runway to be in the first category rather than the second, because the connector will eventually fail, whether anyone inspected it or not.
