## The Decision

I didn't leave the Canadian Armed Forces because I was done with the work. I left because I saw something bigger I could contribute to.

Twenty-one years as a Warrant Officer. Started as an avionics technician on CF-188 Hornets, moved into electronic warfare, then into quality management as an AF9000+ Lead Auditor, and finished supervising a 12-person instruction cell at CFLRS in Saint-Jean-sur-Richelieu. Every one of those roles came back to the same idea: complex systems need rigorous, human-centered oversight to function safely.

In avionics, that meant every wire, every connector, every test procedure had a documented standard, an audit trail, and a trained person responsible for it. In the instruction cell, it meant building curriculum frameworks that could turn raw recruits into competent technicians through a structured, repeatable process.

Then I started watching what was happening with AI.

## The Parallel

Organizations were deploying AI systems with no documentation, no audit trails, no clear accountability, and no training for the people using them. It looked exactly like what happens when you skip quality management in aviation maintenance. Things work fine until they don't. And when they don't, nobody can trace what went wrong or why.

The gap between where AI governance was and where it needed to be looked like a problem I was built to work on. Not because I'm a software engineer. Because I spent two decades learning how to build oversight systems for complex technology, and AI needed that thinking more than anything else.

## The Year in Between

Before I started Nord Paradigm, my wife Audrey, our son Nathaniel, and I spent a year travelling. Sixteen countries. It wasn't a vacation; it was a reset. After 21 years of military structure, I needed to decompress, think, and figure out what I actually wanted to build.

That year did something important. It gave me distance from the institution and clarity about what skills I'd built that had value outside of it. Sitting in airports, watching how different countries organized their systems (immigration processing, public transit, hotel operations), the auditor brain never turned off. I was evaluating everything through the same lens I'd used for maintenance programs.

By the time we got home to Saguenay, I knew exactly what I wanted to do.

## What Transferred

Almost everything. The military doesn't get enough credit for the transferable skills it builds.

Systematic thinking carried over directly. You can't audit a complex system by gut feel. You need frameworks, criteria, and evidence. AI governance requires the same discipline.

Teaching complex concepts simply. The instruction cell taught me to take complicated technical material and make it accessible to people with different backgrounds. That's exactly what AI workshops need.

Comfort with ambiguity. Military operations rarely go as planned. You learn to make decisions with incomplete information, adapt quickly, and maintain standards under pressure. AI adoption is full of the same uncertainty.

Quality culture. In AF9000+ environments, quality isn't a department. It's everyone's job. That mindset is exactly what I try to build in the organizations I work with now.

## What I Built

Nord Paradigm focuses on AI governance auditing, ISO/IEC 42001 compliance, and practical adoption strategies for Quebec and Canadian organizations. I conduct readiness assessments, build governance frameworks, run AI literacy workshops, do impact assessments, and provide advisory services. All grounded in the same principles that kept aircraft systems safe and compliant.

I also built OpenClaw, a six-agent autonomous AI ecosystem running on local infrastructure. Built it in a week starting from zero. Not because a client asked for it, but because I learn by building. The consulting I do is better because I've built things myself.

## The Honest Part

The career pivot wasn't easy. Leaving after 21 years meant walking away from a pension timeline, a community, and an identity I'd had since I was 19. But every conversation I have with a business leader who suddenly understands what AI governance actually means confirms it was the right call.
