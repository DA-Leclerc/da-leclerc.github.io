## The Decision

After 21 years as a Warrant Officer in the Canadian Armed Forces, I didn't leave because I was done with the work. I left because I saw something bigger I could contribute to.

My career in the military was built around a single idea: complex systems need rigorous, human-centered oversight to function safely. In avionics, that meant every wire, every connector, every test procedure had a documented standard, an audit trail, and a trained person responsible for it. When I ran the instruction cell, I was the person making sure everyone else could execute those standards, not just follow them mechanically, but understand why they existed.

That idea (that complex systems need governance, and governance needs people who understand both the system and the humans operating it) is exactly what the AI space is missing.

## What I Noticed

While still serving, I started paying attention to how organizations were adopting AI. What I saw worried me. Not because the technology was dangerous, but because the approach was reckless. Companies were deploying AI tools with no governance framework, no risk assessment, no change management plan, and no training for the people using them.

I'd spent two decades in a world where you don't launch a system until the processes, documentation, and training are in place. Watching businesses launch AI with none of that felt like watching someone skip the pre-flight checklist.

## The Pivot

I didn't quit and figure it out later. I planned the transition while still in uniform. I enrolled in the University of Helsinki's AI ethics course. I studied Quebec's Loi 25 and the proposed federal AIDA. I built an AI readiness assessment framework based on the six domains I'd seen matter most: Leadership, Data, Skills, Governance, Processes, and Innovation.

I taught myself to build AI agents, not because I wanted to become a developer, but because I believe you can't govern what you don't understand. I now use tools like Claude and custom automation pipelines in my daily work. The consulting I do is better because I've built things myself.

## What Transferred

Almost everything. The military doesn't get enough credit for the transferable skills it builds. Here's what carried over directly:

**Systematic thinking.** You can't audit a complex system by gut feel. You need frameworks, criteria, and evidence. AI governance requires the same discipline.

**Teaching complex concepts simply.** The instruction cell taught me to take complicated technical material and make it accessible to people with different backgrounds. That's exactly what AI workshops need.

**Comfort with ambiguity.** Military operations rarely go exactly as planned. You learn to make decisions with incomplete information, adapt quickly, and maintain standards under pressure. AI adoption is full of the same ambiguity.

**Quality culture.** In AF9000+ environments, quality isn't a department. It's everyone's job. That mindset is what I try to build in the organizations I work with. AI governance that lives in a policy document nobody reads is worthless. It needs to be part of how people work.

## What I Had to Learn

Plenty. The regulations were new territory: Loi 25 is specific to Quebec, AIDA is federal, and the EU AI Act has global implications. I had to learn the consulting business itself: pricing, positioning, client management. I had to build a professional network in a completely new field.

And I had to get comfortable with being a beginner again. After two decades of being the expert in the room, starting over was humbling. But the military taught me something useful about that too: competence is built, not born. You show up, you do the work, and you earn the credibility.

## Where I Am Now

I'm based in Saguenay, QC, working with Quebec and Canadian organizations on AI readiness, governance, and compliance. I run workshops, conduct assessments, and provide advisory services, all grounded in the same principles that kept aircraft systems safe and compliant.

The career pivot wasn't easy. But every conversation I have with a business leader who suddenly understands what AI governance actually means, who has that lightbulb moment, confirms it was the right call.
