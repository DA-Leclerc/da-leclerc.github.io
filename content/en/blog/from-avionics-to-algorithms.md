## The Decision

I left the Canadian Armed Forces after twenty-one years as a Warrant Officer, and I didn't leave because I was tired of the work. I left because the work was migrating, and I could see where it was going. My whole career was built around a single idea: complex systems need rigorous, human-centered oversight to function safely, and that oversight has to live inside the daily operating rhythm of the people running the system rather than in a binder on a shelf. In avionics, that meant every wire, every connector, every test procedure had a documented standard, an audit trail, and a trained person responsible for it. When I ran the instruction cell, my job was making sure everyone else could execute those standards not just mechanically but with an understanding of why they existed, because the mechanical version fails the first time conditions change. That same idea, applied to algorithms instead of airframes, is exactly what the current AI wave is missing.

## What I Noticed

While I was still in uniform, I started paying attention to how civilian organizations were adopting AI, and what I saw worried me in a specific way. The technology itself wasn't the problem; the approach was. Companies were deploying AI tools with no governance framework, no risk assessment, no change management plan, and no training for the people who were suddenly using them. I'd spent two decades inside a culture where you don't launch a system until the processes, documentation, and training are in place, and watching organizations skip every one of those steps with a technology they didn't understand felt like watching someone start the engine while the pre-flight checklist was still sitting on the workbench.

## The Pivot

I planned the transition while still serving rather than quitting and figuring it out afterward. I enrolled in the University of Helsinki's AI ethics course, read Quebec's Loi 25 and the proposed federal AIDA end to end, and built an AI readiness assessment framework structured around six domains: leadership, data, skills, governance, processes, and innovation. I also taught myself to build AI agents from scratch, not because I wanted to become a developer but because you cannot meaningfully govern a technology you don't understand at the implementation level. The consulting I do now is better for it, because every client conversation about risk or architecture is grounded in things I've actually built rather than things I've only read about.

## What Transferred

Almost everything, and the military doesn't get enough credit for the transferable skills it builds into the people who stay long enough to absorb them. Systematic thinking is the most obvious one: you cannot audit a complex system on gut feel, you need frameworks, criteria, and evidence, and AI governance demands exactly the same discipline because the failure modes are subtle enough that intuition alone misses them. Teaching complex concepts simply is the second: the instruction cell taught me to take complicated technical material and make it accessible to people with different backgrounds and different levels of prior exposure, which is the core skill behind any workshop that actually changes how participants work on Monday morning. Comfort with ambiguity is the third, because military operations rarely go exactly as planned, and you learn to make decisions with incomplete information, adapt quickly, and maintain standards under pressure. AI adoption is full of the same ambiguity, and clients need advisors who can operate inside it without freezing. And finally, quality culture: in AF9000+ environments, quality is not a department, it's everyone's job, and that mindset is what I try to build inside the organizations I work with. AI governance that lives in a policy document nobody reads is worthless; it has to be part of how people actually work.

## What I Had to Learn

Plenty. The regulations were genuinely new territory, because Loi 25 is specific to Quebec, AIDA was federal and then wasn't, and the EU AI Act has implications that reach into Canadian companies with any European exposure at all. I had to learn the consulting business itself, which is a different trade from the technical work: pricing, positioning, client management, the rhythm of discovery and delivery. I had to build a professional network in a completely new field from a standing start, and I had to get comfortable being a beginner again after two decades of being the expert in the room. The military taught me something useful about that last part too: competence is built, not born, and you earn credibility by showing up, doing the work, and letting the results speak.

## Where I Am Now

I work out of Saguenay with Quebec and Canadian organizations on AI readiness, governance, and compliance, running workshops, conducting assessments, and providing advisory services that are all grounded in the same principles that kept aircraft systems safe. The moments that confirm the pivot was right are the ones where a business leader suddenly understands what AI governance actually means, not as a compliance burden but as the thing that makes the whole adoption strategy survivable. Those conversations are what I left the uniform for.
