## The Parallels Nobody Talks About

When people hear I spent 21 years in the Canadian Armed Forces doing avionics maintenance and quality auditing, and that I now consult on AI governance, the first question is usually some version of: "How did you end up here?"

The honest answer? I didn't change what I do. I changed what I apply it to.

AF9000+ is the Canadian military's quality management standard. Think of it as ISO 9001's stricter cousin, designed for environments where a process failure can kill someone. I spent years as a Lead Auditor in that system, reviewing maintenance programs, training frameworks, and operational procedures for compliance and effectiveness.

Here's what I've learned: every principle that makes AF9000+ work maps directly onto what AI governance needs to be.

## Six Lessons That Carry Over

**1. Document everything or it didn't happen.**

In military maintenance, if you didn't log the inspection, it didn't happen. Doesn't matter if you did the work. No record, no proof, no compliance. AI governance is identical. If you can't show how a model was trained, what data it used, how decisions were validated, and who approved the deployment, you have no governance. You have a PowerPoint deck and a hope.

**2. Build accountability into the structure, not the culture.**

Culture matters. But culture alone doesn't prevent failures. You need defined roles with clear authority, documented approval chains, and consequences for deviation. In AF9000+, every process had an owner, every deviation had a corrective action, and every corrective action had a follow-up. AI governance needs the same structure. Saying "we value responsible AI" means nothing without the mechanisms to enforce it.

**3. Audit the process, not just the output.**

In aviation maintenance, you don't just check whether the aircraft flies. You verify that every step of the maintenance procedure was followed correctly, by qualified people, using approved tools and documentation. AI governance should work the same way. Don't just check whether the model gives good results. Verify the data pipeline, the training process, the validation methodology, and the deployment procedure. A good output from a broken process is a lucky accident.

**4. Train for competence, not compliance.**

When I ran the instruction cell, the goal wasn't producing people who could pass a test. It was producing people who understood the system well enough to catch problems the test didn't cover. AI literacy works the same way. Checkbox training that teaches people to click "I agree" on an AI policy is useless. Your team needs to understand what the AI is doing well enough to catch when it's not.

**5. Match controls to risk.**

Not every maintenance task gets the same level of oversight. A routine inspection and an engine overhaul have very different control requirements. AI governance should work the same way. A chatbot answering FAQs doesn't need the same governance as an AI making credit decisions or screening job applicants. Match the controls to the risk.

**6. Continuous improvement isn't optional.**

AF9000+ isn't a one-time certification. It's a living system with regular audits, corrective actions, and process updates. Your AI governance framework should work the same way. Regulations are changing fast (Loi 25, the EU AI Act), and your processes need to keep up.

## The Bottom Line

AI governance isn't a new discipline. It's risk management, quality assurance, and process engineering applied to a new domain. If your organization already has quality systems, compliance frameworks, or safety protocols, you're closer to good AI governance than you think.

The gap isn't usually knowledge. It's the bridge between what you already know and how to apply it to AI. That's exactly where I work.
