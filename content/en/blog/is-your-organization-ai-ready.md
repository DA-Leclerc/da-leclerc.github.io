## The Question Nobody Asks First

Every week I talk to business leaders who want to "do something with AI." They've seen the headlines, heard competitors are using it, and feel the pressure to move. But when I ask what problem they're trying to solve, the room goes quiet.

That's not a criticism. It's the most common starting point. And it's exactly why a readiness assessment matters before you spend a dollar on AI tools or consulting.

Here are five questions I use to gauge where an organization actually stands. You can answer them yourself in ten minutes.

## 1. Can you name the specific problem AI would solve?

"We want to be more efficient" is not a problem statement. "Our customer service team spends 40% of their time answering the same 15 questions" is.

AI works best when it's pointed at a concrete, measurable problem. If you can't articulate the problem with specifics (what's broken, how much it costs, who it affects), you're not ready to evaluate AI solutions yet. Start there.

## 2. Is your data accessible and organized?

AI runs on data. If your data lives in disconnected spreadsheets, legacy systems that don't talk to each other, or filing cabinets, that's your first project. Not AI. Data infrastructure.

This doesn't mean you need a data lake or a team of engineers. It means you need to know what data you have, where it lives, how clean it is, and whether you can actually access it programmatically. If the honest answer is "I'm not sure," that tells you something useful.

## 3. Who owns AI decisions in your organization?

When an AI system makes a recommendation, who's accountable for what happens next? If the answer is "nobody" or "IT, I guess," that's a gap.

It doesn't need to be a dedicated role. It can be part of an existing mandate. But someone needs to own the decisions, the risks, and the governance around how AI gets used. Without that ownership, AI projects drift, overlap, or quietly create liabilities nobody is tracking.

## 4. Does your team understand what AI can and can't do?

The biggest risk in AI adoption isn't the technology. It's misaligned expectations. Teams that think AI will solve everything are as dangerous as teams that think it's all hype. You need a baseline level of AI literacy across your leadership and key staff.

Here's a quick test. Ask your leadership team two questions: "What's one thing AI could help us with?" and "What's one thing AI shouldn't do in our context?" If the answers are vague or identical across the room, you have a literacy gap that needs addressing before you buy anything.

## 5. Are you prepared to change how people work?

AI adoption is change management. Even the best AI tool fails if people don't trust it, understand it, or integrate it into their daily work. The organizations that succeed with AI treat it as a people project with a technology component, not the other way around.

If your organization has a history of resisting new tools or processes, that pattern will repeat with AI. Address the culture first. Run workshops. Build understanding. Get buy-in from the people who will actually use the thing before you deploy it.

## What Your Answers Tell You

If you scored well on all five, you're in a strong position to start evaluating specific AI solutions. Most organizations don't. And that's fine. Knowing where the gaps are is the whole point.
