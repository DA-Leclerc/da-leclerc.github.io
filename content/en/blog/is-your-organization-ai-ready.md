Every week I have a version of the same conversation with a business leader who is convinced their organization needs AI but cannot describe, in any concrete terms, what problem the investment is supposed to solve. They've sat through the vendor demos, heard the pitches, maybe approved a budget line on the strength of a McKinsey chart, and now they're looking for a consultant to validate a decision they've already made. The hard questions about whether the foundation can actually support what they're trying to build rarely come up, because those questions are uncomfortable, and the five that follow are the ones I've learned to ask before anything else.

## The Problem You Think You're Solving

"We need AI" is not a strategy, it's a reaction to market pressure, and the organizations that skip past the problem definition step are the ones whose pilots die quietly twelve months in. Before spending a dollar on tools or consultants, a company needs a specific, measurable problem that AI could plausibly address better than the current approach, and the test for specificity is whether the problem can be written as a sentence with numbers in it. "We spend four hundred hours per month manually classifying customer complaints, and the accuracy rate is seventy percent" is a real problem; "we want to use AI to improve customer experience" is a press release. Most organizations start from the technology and hunt for problems to attach it to, which is exactly backward, and the resulting projects tend to produce impressive demos that never cross the threshold into daily operations.

## Whether the Data Is Actually Ready

AI runs on data, which is the most over-quoted observation in the field and somehow still the thing that blindsides the most executives. If the data underneath a proposed AI system is scattered across disconnected platforms, inconsistently formatted, full of gaps, or poorly documented, no vendor tool is going to rescue the project, because the vendor's algorithms will inherit every one of those problems and amplify them. The honest diagnostic is to ask where the data actually lives, whether it's accessible, whether it's labelled and structured consistently, whether there's enough of it for the targeted use case, and who owns it along with the associated privacy exposure. Organizations that cannot answer those questions clearly are not ready for AI; they are ready for a data cleanup project, and the data cleanup project is almost always the more valuable investment anyway.

## Whether Leadership Understands What It's Approving

AI adoption without executive understanding produces misaligned expectations, and misaligned expectations kill more projects than technical failures do. If a CEO thinks AI is magic and an IT team knows it's applied statistics, the communication gap between them will eventually become a budgeting gap, a timeline gap, and then a finger-pointing exercise. Leadership doesn't need to understand transformer architectures, but it does need to understand what AI can realistically do, what the total cost of ownership looks like beyond the license fee, what the failure modes are, and what governance means in practice. The quick test is whether an executive sponsor can explain the project's purpose and limitations in plain language without reaching for a slide deck; if they can't, the sponsorship is nominal and the project has no one defending it when the first hard trade-off shows up.

## Who Owns the Failures

This is the question nobody wants to answer, because the honest answer requires naming a person, and the easy answer (the vendor) does not survive contact with reality. AI systems make mistakes: they produce biased outputs, they hallucinate, they break in ways nobody predicted, and when any of those things happens in production, somebody inside the organization needs to own the response. "Nobody" and "the vendor" are both governance gaps, and the organizations that answer this question clearly before deployment (with defined roles, escalation procedures, and monitoring processes) are the ones that avoid the expensive surprises. The ones that haven't answered it usually discover the gap the hard way, on a Tuesday afternoon, when a customer complaint forces it into the open.

## Whether the People Are Ready for the Change

AI changes workflows and the texture of day-to-day work, and if a deployment lands on a team that hasn't been prepared for the impact, adoption fails regardless of how good the underlying technology is. The preparation involves training, obviously, but it also involves change management in the broader sense: communicating why the change is happening, involving the people who will use the tools in the design decisions, addressing fears honestly rather than dismissing them, and creating feedback loops so employees can flag issues before they compound. The best AI deployments I've seen are the ones where the team felt like participants in the rollout; the worst are the ones where the tool appeared one Monday with a training video attached.

If an organization can answer all five of these questions honestly and clearly, it's probably ready for a serious AI initiative and should stop reading blog posts and start scoping the work. If two or more of the questions produced discomfort, the readiness gap itself is the first project, and trying to skip past it into a tool selection process is the most expensive mistake in the current AI adoption cycle.
